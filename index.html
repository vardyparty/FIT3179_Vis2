Venues and Attendances in the Australian Football League, in the AFL Era (2012-present)

Some of the most attended matches feature Melburnian teams, with local rivalries, clashes with history, and big capacity stadiums setting the scene for spectators to descend upon football heartland by the thousands. Annual games such as the ANZAC day match between Collingwood and Essendon, and the Good Friday Superclash between North Melbourne and Carlton - regularly draw in excess of 30 thousand attendees. While Melburnian teams, such as Collingwood, Melbourne, and Richmond tend to draw large crowds, signalled by dark blue gradients, emerging teams such as Gold Coast and Greater Western Sydney struggle to attract many spectators, especially at home. This is indicated by numerous squares with lighter blue gradients. Note: as the MCG, a Melbourne stadium, is the only >60 thousand capacity stadium in Australia regularly hosting Australian Football matches, it becomes difficult for interstate clubs to draw crowds in excess of 60 thousand. However, as future diagrams will show, having a large stadium capacity does not guarantee a relatively large turnout.

Since 2012, games in the Australian Football League (AFL) has been played all around the country, from Darwin to Hobart, and from Perth to Townsville. Despite this, the main trend that AFL fixturing follows favours the major cities - after all, they are where teams' home matches are predominantly played, with venues in other towns only seeing the occasional match. These may be annual matches, such as Melbourne's annual game at TIO Stadium, Alice Springs, or matches due to unforeseen circumstances, such as the large number of games played at Cairns' Cazaly Stadium due to COVID-19 restrictions.
